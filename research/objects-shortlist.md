# Objects shortlist — 15 Kali + 15 Paradevi, paired

Author: PlayfulProcess. Status: seed shortlist, 2026-07-02.

Facts below are deliberately basic and mainstream (type, order-of-magnitude distance, one famous verified feature). Distances are approximate; quasar/deep-field distances are light-travel figures. Verify each fact against the mission source (NASA/ESA/ESO/EHT) when the item enters a grammar.

## Kali deck — black holes, voids, dark mass

| # | Object | Type | Distance | Constellation / direction | One striking fact |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| K1 | Sagittarius A* (Sgr A*) | Supermassive black hole | ~26,000 ly | Sagittarius | ~4 million solar masses at the center of our own galaxy; imaged by the EHT in 2022; its discovery earned the 2020 physics Nobel |
| K2 | M87* | Supermassive black hole | ~55 million ly | Virgo | The first black hole ever imaged (EHT, 2019); ~6.5 billion solar masses; launches a jet thousands of light-years long |
| K3 | Cygnus X-1 | Stellar-mass black hole (X-ray binary) | ~7,000 ly | Cygnus | The first widely accepted black hole; subject of a famous Hawking-Thorne bet; it darkens and feeds on a blue supergiant companion |
| K4 | TON 618 | Ultramassive black hole (quasar) | ~10 billion ly | Canes Venatici | Among the most massive black holes known, estimated in the tens of billions of solar masses |
| K5 | V404 Cygni | Black hole X-ray binary | ~8,000 ly | Cygnus | Slept for 26 years, then erupted in 2015 in one of the brightest X-ray outbursts ever seen |
| K6 | Gaia BH1 | Dormant stellar-mass black hole | ~1,600 ly | Ophiuchus | The nearest known black hole to Earth (announced 2022); found not by light but by a Sun-like star's wobble |
| K7 | GW150914 | Binary black hole merger (gravitational-wave event) | ~1.3 billion ly | Southern sky (poorly localized) | The first gravitational waves ever detected (Sept 2015); two black holes became one, radiating ~3 solar masses as pure spacetime ripples |
| K8 | Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-56) | Colliding galaxy clusters — dark mass | ~3.7 billion ly | Carina | The collision separated dark matter from hot gas — a direct empirical portrait of mass we cannot see |
| K9 | Boötes Void | Supervoid | ~700 million ly | Boötes | Roughly 330 million light-years of near-nothing; only a few dozen galaxies known inside "the Great Nothing" |
| K10 | Local Void | Void | Adjacent to the Local Group | Toward Aquila | An enormous emptiness beginning at our galactic doorstep; our galaxy is moving away from it |
| K11 | Great Attractor | Mass concentration | ~150-250 million ly | Toward Norma / Centaurus | An unseen pull dragging the Milky Way and thousands of galaxies toward it, hidden behind our own galactic plane |
| K12 | 3C 273 | Quasar | ~2.4 billion ly | Virgo | The first object recognized as a quasar (1963); the optically brightest quasar in our sky — a "star" that was never a star |
| K13 | OJ 287 | Binary supermassive black hole (blazar) | ~3.5 billion ly | Cancer | Flares on a ~12-year cycle, read as a smaller black hole plunging through the disk of a giant one — a clock made of orbits |
| K14 | GRS 1915+105 | Microquasar (black hole binary) | ~30,000 ly | Aquila | A heavyweight stellar black hole firing jets at close to light speed, with wildly erratic X-ray "heartbeats" |
| K15 | Cygnus A | Radio galaxy (active black hole) | ~760 million ly | Cygnus | One of the loudest radio sources in the sky; its jets inflate lobes far larger than the galaxy itself |

## Paradevi deck — galaxies, radiant structure

| # | Object | Type | Distance | Constellation | One striking fact |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| P1 | Milky Way | Barred spiral galaxy | Home | All-sky (center in Sagittarius) | Our own radiance: on the order of 100-400 billion stars, seen from inside as a river of light |
| P2 | Andromeda (M31) | Spiral galaxy | ~2.5 million ly | Andromeda | Nearest large galaxy; the object Hubble used (1920s) to prove other "island universes" exist; on a slow approach to the Milky Way |
| P3 | Triangulum (M33) | Spiral galaxy | ~2.7 million ly | Triangulum | Third-largest galaxy of the Local Group; a quiet neighbor visible to sharp naked eyes on dark nights |
| P4 | Sombrero (M104) | Spiral galaxy | ~30 million ly | Virgo | A luminous bulge wearing a dramatic dark dust lane — light and shadow in a single body |
| P5 | Cartwheel Galaxy | Ring galaxy | ~500 million ly | Sculptor | A smaller galaxy plunged through its heart; the splash became an expanding ring of newborn stars |
| P6 | Antennae Galaxies (NGC 4038/4039) | Colliding galaxy pair | ~60 million ly | Corvus | Two galaxies mid-merger, throwing out long tidal "antennae" and igniting furious star formation |
| P7 | GN-z11 | Early galaxy | ~13.4 billion ly (light-travel) | Ursa Major | Among the earliest galaxies confirmed — its light left roughly 400 million years after the Big Bang |
| P8 | Whirlpool (M51) | Grand-design spiral + companion | ~30 million ly | Canes Venatici | The first galaxy in which spiral structure was seen (Lord Rosse, 1845); holds hands with its small companion NGC 5195 |
| P9 | Pinwheel (M101) | Face-on spiral galaxy | ~21 million ly | Ursa Major | A vast face-on disk larger than the Milky Way, yet so diffuse it hides from small telescopes |
| P10 | Large Magellanic Cloud | Satellite galaxy | ~160,000 ly | Dorado / Mensa | Hosts the Tarantula Nebula, the most furious star-forming region in our neighborhood, and Supernova 1987A |
| P11 | Small Magellanic Cloud | Satellite galaxy | ~200,000 ly | Tucana | Henrietta Leavitt's variable stars here became the ruler by which we measured the universe |
| P12 | Hoag's Object | Ring galaxy | ~600 million ly | Serpens | A near-perfect ring of blue stars around a golden core, with an apparent gap between them — a halo with a held breath |
| P13 | Sculptor Galaxy (NGC 253) | Starburst spiral | ~11 million ly | Sculptor | One of the brightest nearby galaxies, forging stars so fast it drives glowing winds out of its own disk |
| P14 | Centaurus A | Peculiar galaxy (nearest active galaxy) | ~12 million ly | Centaurus | A luminous elliptical wrapped in a dark dust band, the nearest galaxy with an actively feeding central black hole |
| P15 | Coma Cluster | Galaxy cluster | ~320 million ly | Coma Berenices | Thousands of galaxies; here Fritz Zwicky (1933) first inferred that unseen mass outweighs the light |

### Notes for the builder

- GW150914 is an *event*, not a persistent object; if the deck wants only standing objects, swap in Cygnus X-3 or the "Unicorn" candidate — but the event's one-time-ness is itself a strong Kali theme (some things happen once and change everything).
- Boötes Void, Local Void, Great Attractor and the two clusters stretch "black hole" into "void/dark-mass phenomena" — intentional, per the founding brief.
- All 30 objects have excellent public imagery: EHT (K1, K2), Chandra/NASA composites (K3, K5, K8, K14, K15), ESO/ESA/Hubble/JWST (nearly all Paradevi items, incl. a famous JWST Cartwheel image).
